375 Studies Changed How I Think About Hiring Therapists

Cuijpers et al. 2025, JAMA Psychiatry
JAMA Psychiatry just published the largest CBT meta-analysis in history — 375 randomized controlled trials, 32,968 patients. The finding that stopped me: CBT effect sizes vary by 4x depending on the condition being treated. PTSD: 1.27 (strong). Bipolar: 0.31 (limited). Anxiety and depression: somewhere in between. For years, the hiring standard in mental health has been: "Do they do CBT?" Check. Next question. But if the data shows a 4x gap in outcomes depending on the match — we've been asking the wrong question. At MMHC, we now hire for condition-specific expertise, not just modality. We ask: "When is CBT NOT the right choice for your patients?" And if they can't answer, that tells us something. 80+ therapists. Multiple modalities. The Control Group Problem in Therapy Marketing

Cuijpers et al. 2025, JAMA Psychiatry
Every therapy practice says "evidence-based." It's become meaningless. Here's why: when a study says "therapy works," the comparison group changes everything. Therapy vs. waitlist? Of course it works better than nothing. Therapy vs. active placebo? Now we're talking. Therapy A vs. Therapy B for a specific condition? That's the data that actually helps patients choose. The JAMA meta-analysis of 375 trials gets specific: CBT effect size of 1.27 for PTSD. Just 0.31 for bipolar. These aren't the same thing, but both get labeled "evidence-based." As a practice leader, I think we have a responsibility to be more honest about what the evidence actually shows — and doesn't show. "Evidence-based" should mean: "Here's the specific evidence for this specific approach with your specific condition." Anything less is marketing, not medicine. What does "evidence-based" actually mean at your organization?
